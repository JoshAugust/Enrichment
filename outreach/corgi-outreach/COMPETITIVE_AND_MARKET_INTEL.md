## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Corgi's GPU RVG product sits in an **effectively unoccupied niche**. There is no standalone, branded, dedicated GPU residual value insurance product available in the market today. The closest analogues are:
1. RVI embedded within GPU ABS securitisation structures (used by sophisticated capital markets, not available as a standalone lender product)
2. Traditional equipment RVI writers (RVI Group, Tokio Marine HCC) who *could* write GPU RVI but have not built the vertical-specific capability
3. A new Lloyd's-backed AI infrastructure facility (ATA, Jan 2026) covering physical damage/cyber but **not** residual value/collateral floors

This is the same white space that RVI Group occupied in 1989 when it invented aircraft RVI. Corgi has the opportunity to be the RVI Group of the GPU age.

### 1.2 Key Finding: No Direct Competitor Exists
A standalone, purpose-built GPU Residual Value Guarantee product — sold to GPU *lenders* to protect against collateral value floors at loan maturity — **does not currently exist as a commercial product**.

**Corgi has first-mover advantage.** The question is how long before RVI Group, TMHCC, or a Lloyd's syndicate replicates this.

## 2. THE AIRCRAFT RVI STORY — CREDIBILITY ANALOG

*Use this narrative to answer the "has this ever been done before?" objection.*

### 2.1 How Aircraft RVI Was Born (1989)

**The origin:** In 1989, Howard Chickering and Thomas R. Cox III founded **R.V.I. Guaranty Co., Ltd. (RVIG)** in Bermuda — deliberately structured as a Bermuda domiciled insurer to capture accounting and regulatory flexibility. The company's founding thesis was simple: aviation finance was growing rapidly, aircraft are expensive long-lived assets, and lessors/lenders had no way to hedge the risk that an aircraft would be worth less than expected at lease-end.

RVIG wrote the world's first aircraft RVI policy with Cessna Financial. At the time, there was no data, no loss history, and no precedent. Sound familiar?

### 2.2 The Journey to Industry Standard

**Phase 1 — Establishing the concept (1989-early 1990s):**
- RVI had to educate the market: what is residual value risk? Why can't you price it in the lease rate alone?
- First clients were sophisticated aviation lessors who understood the risk but had no tool to manage it
- RVIG built proprietary databases and valuation models — the underwriting *was* the moat

**Phase 2 — Aviation finance sophistication creates demand (mid-1990s):**
- The first EETC (Enhanced Equipment Trust Certificate) was done by Northwest Airlines in **1994** — the first time aircraft were securitised in capital markets structures
- EETC structures required rating agency confidence in future aircraft values → created demand for third-party RVI to bridge the confidence gap
- By the late 1990s, over $42 billion in EETCs had been issued with aircraft as collateral
- RVI moved from "nice to have" to a structural necessity in certain aviation finance transactions

**Phase 3 — Standard product (2000s–present):**
- RVI Group has now insured **over $59 billion in residuals since 1989**
- Ratings: **A- (Excellent) from AM Best, BBB+ from S&P** — achieved through 30+ years of claims-free track record and disciplined underwriting
- Little meaningful competition emerged — the barriers to entry (data, models, relationships) proved durable
- AGRO (Assured Guaranty Re Overseas) later entered the aircraft RVI market as a reinsurer to provide additional capacity, confirming the market was real

### 2.3 The Parallel to GPU RVG

| Aircraft RVI (1989) | GPU RVG (2025) |
|---|---|
| New asset class being debt-financed at scale | New asset class being debt-financed at scale |
| No loss history, no price data | No loss history, limited price data (5 years) |
| Lenders unsure of collateral floor | Lenders unsure of collateral floor — 50-70% advance rates reflect uncertainty |
| EETCs demanded structural credit enhancement | GPU ABS demanded structural RVI — already used in first $1.1B GPU ABS deal |
| One company had the courage to write it first | Corgi is writing it first |
| RVI Group built the data moat, became the standard | Corgi builds the data moat, becomes the standard |

**Key pitch line:** *"RVI Group didn't have 30 years of aircraft data in 1989 either. They built the market."*

### 3.3 Historical Analog: A100 Depreciation After H100 Launch

The A100 launched ~2020 at ~$10,000–$15,000 (OEM). When H100 shipped in volume (2023):
- **A100 dropped to ~$7,000–$15,000 new; $4,000–$9,000 used** by early 2026
- That's a **40–60% decline in market value** over 3 years from successor launch
- **However:** NVIDIA's CFO noted A100s shipped 6 years ago are *still running at full utilisation* for inference workloads — the "value cascade" model

**Value Cascade Model (use in pitch):**
- **Years 1-2:** Frontier training workloads (maximum performance premium)
- **Years 3-4:** High-value real-time inference (previous-gen suffices)
- **Years 5-6:** Batch inference and analytics

This is the bull case for GPU value retention. The risk case is: if inference demand doesn't materialise, or commodity cloud pricing continues to collapse, the cascade stalls.

### 3.4 Blackwell (H200/B100/B200) Impact on H100 Values

- H200 (incremental over H100): +15–20% premium vs H100; now pressuring H100 used prices
- B200 (Blackwell architecture): GA in Q1 2026; historical patterns suggest **10–20% price reduction for previous-gen hardware** as enterprises upgrade
- Rubin-architecture GPUs (late 2026–2027): Expected to **sharply pressure H100/H200 values; potential 50-60% decline within 6 months of widespread Rubin availability**
- Counter-signal: Blackwell B200/GB200 is **sold out through mid-2026** with 3.6M unit backlog — demand may sustain H100 inference values while new supply is constrained

**Key risk for RVG underwriting:** A 3-year loan written today against H100s faces material residual value risk in 2028–2029 when Rubin is mainstream.

## 4. GPU ABS MARKET

### 4.1 The First GPU ABS — $1.1 Billion (Early 2025)

- **First-ever ABS backed by high-end AI chips**, launched early 2025
- **Size:** $1.1 billion ("shelf financing")
- **Rating:** AAA on senior notes
- **Spread:** Priced inside 110 basis points — tighter than typical high-quality corporate debt
- **Issuer:** Not publicly disclosed (distinct from CoreWeave's term loan facilities)
- **Demand:** Oversubscribed — investors "snapped up" the bonds

**How the structure worked:**
- GPUs bundled as collateral pool (hundreds/thousands of H100s in data centers)
- UCC-1 liens filed against each GPU by serial number
- Revenue: Lease payments / pay-per-use compute fees flow to SPV
- Credit enhancement: ~19% subordination + **residual value insurance guaranteeing 40% floor on GPU values**
- Anticipated Repayment Date: 3–4 years (aligned to GPU useful life)
- Final legal maturity: 7–10 years

**The RVI math in the deal:**
"Suppose a $81M AAA tranche against $100M of GPUs. Rating agency stress: ~6.6% pool losses. Insurer guarantees 40% residual value. Even if used GPU market collapses 60%, insurance reimburses to the 40% floor. Combined with 50% amortisation by GPU maturity, the structure achieves AAA." (Source: Medium/@Elongated_musk)

### 4.3 Rating Agency Positions on GPU Residual Value

**S&P Global:**
- Previously capped data center ABS at A+ (pre-2025) due to "short sector history"
- Now willing to rate GPU ABS higher with sufficient credit enhancement

**Moody's:**
- Published data center ABS rating methodology in 2025
- **No hard ratings cap** — willing to achieve AAA with robust structure
- Stress case assumption: ~6.6% pool losses in downturn scenario
- Key variable: residual value uncertainty; RVI is the structural solution

**Kroll / DBRS:**
- Approaching GPU collateral conservatively; advance rate assumptions influence required enhancement
- No published depreciation curve specific to GPUs (as of early 2026)

**Rating agency key quote:** Until RVI exists as a standalone product, every GPU ABS deal that needs AAA must solve the residual value problem structurally (overcollateralisation + subordination). RVI shrinks the required credit enhancement — lowering the issuer's cost of capital.

## 5. RISK ASSESSMENT INDUSTRY VIEW

### 5.1 What Lenders Are Doing

- **Advance rates:** 50–70% LTV (vs. 80–95% for houses, 70–90% for cars, 80–90% for heavy equipment)
- **Interest rates:** 12–15% for early deals; more recent deals at SOFR + 4-6% (still double hyperscaler rates)
- **Current GPU-backed debt outstanding:** $20B+ (four firms alone exceed $20B); private credit to AI surged from near zero to >$200B "in just a few years" (Morgan Stanley)
- **Structural protection:** Lenders require bankruptcy-remote SPVs, serial-number-level UCC-1 liens, controlled data center custody

**The Lending Spectrum (from Cash & Cache "GPU Mortgage" piece):**
| Asset | LTV Available | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Houses | 80–95% | 100 years of data; appreciates |
| Cars | 70–90% | Predictable depreciation curve |
| Heavy equipment | 70–90% | Liquid secondary market (auction) |
| IT servers (hyperscaler) | ~100% (leases) | Essential use, lessee credit |
| GPUs | 50–70% | No historical data; volatile obsolescence |

**The thesis for RVG:** Move GPUs from "50–70% LTV" to "80–85% LTV" by insuring the residual value floor. Every percentage point of additional LTV on the $200B+ GPU lending book is a massive market.

### 5.2 What's Driving Conservative Pricing

1. **No historical depreciation data** — GPU-as-collateral market barely existed 5 years ago
2. **NVIDIA annual product cadence** — new architectures every 12–18 months create genuine obsolescence risk
3. **Liquidation risk** — in enforcement scenarios, forced selling of large GPU portfolios could crater secondary market prices (correlated collateral risk)
4. **Demand uncertainty** — if AI inference demand stalls, utilisation drops, and GPU collateral values fall
5. **Concentration risk** — NVIDIA dominates; AMD/Intel alternatives not yet equivalent

### 5.3 ELFA / Industry Body Context

- ELFA (Equipment Leasing and Finance Association) has begun covering GPU lending: member Wingspire Equipment Finance closed a $65M+ GPU deal (Blackwell architecture) in June 2025 — reported on ELFA's own news feed
- Total equipment leasing market on trajectory to exceed $1 trillion; ELFA views GPU finance as a new frontier sub-segment
- No published ELFA depreciation standards or GPU-specific guidance yet — the industry is making it up as it goes

### 5.4 Analyst / Short-Seller Concerns

- **Jim Chanos** (noted short-seller): "There's going to be debt defaults" — explicitly warning on GPU-backed debt
- **Michael Burry**: Expressed concern about GPU depreciation in public comments; NVIDIA's CFO rebutted citing A100 utilisation data
- **Princeton CITP (Dec 2025 paper):** "AI Chip Lifespans: A Note on the Secondary Market" — academic analysis of GPU depreciation dynamics
