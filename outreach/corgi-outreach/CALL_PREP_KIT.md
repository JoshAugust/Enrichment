## MINUTES 0–2: THE OPENING

**First 60 seconds — what to say (verbatim):**

> *"Thanks for making time — I'll keep this tight to your 20 minutes. Quick context on me: I'm Josh, founder of Corgi Insurance. We built a product specifically for GPU lenders — it's called a Residual Value Guarantee. The short version is: we put a floor on the value of GPU collateral at loan maturity. If the market drops below that floor, we pay the lender the difference. It's a structure that's been used in aviation finance for 35 years — we're the first to bring it to GPU lending.*
>
> *Before I go any further — I'd rather spend the first few minutes understanding your book. Would that be alright?"*

**Why this works:**
- Establishes product in 15 seconds
- Deploys the aviation analogy early (credibility anchor)
- Immediately signals you're there to listen, not pitch
- Gives them control — they relax

**Rapport micro-move (if there's any small talk):**
- Reference their recent deal or announcement if you know it
- Keep it one sentence max — they booked this call for a reason

## MINUTES 2–5: DISCOVERY

**Goal:** Get them talking. Listen for: deal sizes, current risk language, how they think about depreciation, whether credit committee is involved.

**The transition line:**
> *"So tell me — how are you currently underwriting GPU collateral risk? Specifically the residual value question at loan maturity."*

Then: **shut up and listen.** Take notes.

**Follow-up probes (use 1–2, don't interrogate):**
- *"When you're pricing a deal, how much of a depreciation haircut are you building in upfront?"*
- *"Has depreciation risk ever caused you to pass on a deal you otherwise liked?"*

- *"How do your credit docs currently treat collateral value at maturity?"*

**What you're listening for:**
- ✅ "We take a big haircut" → pain confirmed, pitch hard
- ✅ "We model it ourselves" → objection is coming, prep response
- ✅ "Borrowers want 3-year deals but we only do 18 months" → PERFECT opening
- ✅ "We passed on a deal because we couldn't get comfortable with the depreciation" → pull this thread

# SECTION 3 — THE PITCH SCRIPT (WORD FOR WORD)

*Target: 7 minutes. Conversational delivery — not a deck read. Use this as a foundation, not a teleprompter.*

---

**THE PROBLEM:**

> *"Here's what I see happening in GPU lending right now — and tell me if this tracks with your experience.*
>
> *GPU lenders are sitting on a collateral problem that's getting harder to ignore. H100s peaked at $40,000 per card in late 2023. Today they're trading in the $6,000 to $15,000 range on the secondary market — depending on condition and vintage. That's an 60-85% decline in three years. Rental rates followed the same curve — peak $12/hour, now $2-4/hour.*
>
> *When you write an 18-month GPU loan, you're making a bet on where that collateral lands. And that bet gets harder to make confidently with every new NVIDIA generation. Blackwell is already compressing H100 values. Whatever comes after Blackwell will do the same to B200s. The depreciation curve for AI compute isn't slowing down — it's arguably accelerating.*
>
> *So the question for every GPU lender is: how do you price that risk? You can discount it upfront — take a big haircut on advance rates. You can cap your tenors at 12 or 18 months. Or you can absorb it as a portfolio risk and hope the secondary market holds. None of those feel great."*

---

**THE SOLUTION:**

> *"What we built at Corgi is a Residual Value Guarantee — an RVG.*
>
> *Here's how it works: at deal origination, you and Corgi agree on a floor value for the GPU collateral at the loan's maturity date. Could be 50 cents on the dollar for a 24-month deal. Could be 60 cents for 18 months — the floor is actuarially priced based on real-time secondary market data.*
>
> *If the market value of the GPUs at maturity is above the floor — great, no claim, you recover in full anyway. If it's below — Corgi pays you the difference. Period. You get the floor, regardless of what the secondary market does.*
>
> *The pricing engine behind this uses SiliconNavigator — Silicon Data's real-time GPU secondary market data platform, backed by 3.5 million data points across 50+ GPU types. This isn't a number we pull from a spreadsheet — it's live market intelligence. And the policy is reinsurance-backed, so you're not taking counterparty risk on a startup.*
>
> *We're covering H100, H200, and Blackwell today. Minimum $1M asset value.*"*

---

**THE LENDER BENEFIT:**

> *"What this changes for you is meaningful.*
>
> *First: **advance rates.** If the floor is guaranteed, you can lend against it. Instead of a 50% advance on GPUs because you're scared of where they land, you can go 70-75% — because the downside is capped. That's a better deal for your borrowers and more volume for you.*
>
> *Second: **tenor.** Right now most GPU lenders won't go past 24 months because the residual value uncertainty is too wide. With a floor in place, 36-month deals become underwritable. That's a meaningful competitive advantage — borrowers want longer terms.*
>
> *Third: **capital treatment.** NAIC explicitly recognizes RVI as acceptable risk mitigation — the same treatment that's been standard in commercial real estate lending for years. If you're an insurance company lender, there's a direct capital benefit.*
>
> *Practically: you're not carrying the residual value risk anymore. You've transferred it to an insurer. Your credit committee has something concrete to underwrite against."*

**THE ANALOGY:**

> *"None of this is new. This is exactly how aviation finance works.*
>
> *Aircraft are expensive, depreciate unpredictably, and get replaced by newer models on 10-15 year cycles. Sound familiar? The aviation finance industry solved this in 1989 with Residual Value Insurance. Today RVI Group alone has insured over $59 billion in aircraft residuals. BBB+/stable from S&P. It's a standard tool in every aircraft lease and loan structure.*

### OBJECTION 1: "You're too new / unproven"

**The objection:**
*"This sounds interesting but you're a new company. We can't build a credit thesis on a startup insurer."*

**The response:**
> *"That's a completely fair concern and I want to address it directly. Two things: First, the policy is reinsurance-backed — meaning the financial exposure sits with an established reinsurer, not just Corgi. The reinsurer is the counterparty risk you're actually underwriting.*
>
> *Second, we're not asking you to bet the portfolio on us. I'm asking for one deal — a small one if you prefer — that we can run a proof of concept on. Use it in your credit committee to establish the framework. That's how every new instrument enters a market. It's how aviation RVI started in 1989 with a single Cessna deal."*

**The pivot:**
> *"If counterparty concerns are the primary thing, the fastest way to get past that is an intro call with our reinsurer. Would that help move things forward?"*

---

### OBJECTION 2: "We already model depreciation ourselves"

**The objection:**
*"We have our own models for GPU depreciation. We build it into the advance rate."*

**The response:**
> *"I hear that — and your models are probably good. But there's a difference between modeling a risk and transferring it. When you model depreciation and take a haircut on advance rates, you're not eliminating the risk — you're pricing it. The risk is still on your balance sheet. If H100 values fall faster than your model predicted — which they did from 2023 to 2025 — you're exposed.*
>
> *RVG doesn't replace your model. It guarantees the outcome your model is pointing at. You pick the floor. You price it into the loan. And now the floor is a contractual guarantee, not a statistical estimate."*

**The pivot:**
> *"What would it take for your credit committee to be willing to increase advance rates by 10-15%? Because that's the direct business impact if residual risk is insured — not modeled."*

---

> *"Would it make sense to bring your compliance team and our reinsurer on a follow-up call? That would let you get the direct answers on licensing, ratings, and counterparty standing from the source."*

The premium pays for itself in deal economics."*

**The pivot:**
> *"The cleanest way to answer this precisely is to run a real deal. Give me one asset pool and a target structure, and I'll come back with a specific indicative premium. That number will tell us both whether the economics work."*
