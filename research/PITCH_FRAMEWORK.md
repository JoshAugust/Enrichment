# 1. Executive Summary

## GPU Residual Value Guarantee Insurance — The Opportunity

### What We Do
Corgi Insurance writes **Residual Value Guarantee (RVG) policies on GPU infrastructure** — the NVIDIA H100s, B200s, and GB200s that power every AI model on earth. Our policy guarantees a minimum future value for GPU hardware, paying out the deficiency if market value falls below the insured floor at a defined future date.

### Market Size
- **$90–120B** in GPU hardware currently sits on corporate balance sheets globally
- **$14.3B+** in disclosed GPU-backed debt deals already closed (CoreWeave alone: $12.4B+)
- **$660–690B** in combined AI infrastructure capex committed for 2026 by the Big 5 alone
- NVIDIA shipped **$130.5B** in data center GPUs in FY2025 (+114% YoY) — every chip is a potential insurable asset
- The addressable RVG premium pool is **$1.2–5B/year** today, growing 30–50% annually with GPU capex

### Why Now
Three forces converged in 2024–2026 that created this market from nothing:

1. **GPUs became collateral.** CoreWeave borrowed $12.4B against NVIDIA GPUs. Lambda securitized $500M in GPU-backed ABS. Apollo structured a $5.4B triple-net GPU lease for xAI. Lenders *require* insurance on this collateral — and no standalone RVG product exists.

2. **GPU ABS hit capital markets.** The first AAA-rated GPU-backed ABS closed at $1.1B in early 2025, with a **40% residual value floor** provided by insurers. This is the structural proof that GPU RVG works — and the market wants more of it.

3. **Depreciation uncertainty is massive.** Companies depreciate GPUs anywhere from 3 to 7 years. Amazon *shortened* its useful life assumption to 5 years in Feb 2025 — the only hyperscaler to do so — explicitly citing AI hardware obsolescence. The gap between accounting book value and actual market value is the risk RVG was built to cover.

### Competitive Advantage
- **No standalone commercial GPU RVG product exists.** Tokio Marine HCC and RVI Group write equipment RVG for aircraft, vehicles, and industrial equipment — but nobody has purpose-built it for GPUs.
- **We're building the data moat.** GPU condition scoring (thermal history, utilization, memory health), secondary market price indexing, and depreciation curve modeling by GPU model/vintage — this is our Cirium Ascend equivalent for silicon.
- **First-mover in a $1B+ annual premium market** with 30–50% annual growth driven by GPU capex expansion.

# 3. Objection Handling

## 3A. Objections from GPU Operators / Cloud Companies

### 1. "We don't need insurance — our GPUs will hold value because AI demand is only going up."

**Counter:** That's exactly what everyone said about real estate in 2007. GPU values are tied to NVIDIA's product roadmap — every new generation (Hopper → Blackwell → Rubin) makes the prior generation worth less. Amazon shortened their depreciation to 5 years in Feb 2025 specifically because AI hardware obsolescence is accelerating. RVG isn't betting against your GPUs — it's protecting your balance sheet against the one scenario you can't control: what NVIDIA ships next.

### 2. "We self-insure our equipment."

**Counter:** Self-insurance works until it doesn't. CoreWeave has $14.6B in GPU assets on their balance sheet. If a generational shift hits and residual values drop 40%, that's a $5.8B write-down absorbed entirely by equity. RVG transfers that tail risk for a fraction of the cost. More importantly, your *lenders* may require it — every major GPU-backed debt deal already includes casualty/theft insurance requirements. RVG is the next layer lenders will mandate.

### 3. "Our lenders haven't asked for this."

**Counter:** They will. The first AAA-rated GPU ABS already includes a 40% residual value floor from insurers. That structure is becoming the template. As GPU-backed lending matures (now $14.3B+ in disclosed deals), lenders will standardize RVG requirements just like aircraft lenders standardized RVI decades ago. Being ahead of that curve means better financing terms today.

### 4. "GPUs depreciate too fast — insurance would be too expensive."

**Counter:** Shorter asset lives actually make RVG *easier* to price — less time horizon means less uncertainty. Aircraft RVI covers 15-year terms on assets worth $50M+. We're covering 2–5 year terms. Our premiums reflect the actual risk, and we use conservative attachment points (30–50% of current value) so we're insuring the floor, not the full value. At 4–10% of guaranteed amount, the cost is a rounding error on your cost of capital.

### 5. "We'd rather spend that premium on more GPUs."

**Counter:** Fair — but consider the leverage. A $10M RVG policy on a $200M GPU fleet could unlock an additional $50M+ in debt capacity at better rates, because your lender's LGD drops. The ROI on the premium isn't the insurance payout — it's the financing arbitrage. CoreWeave didn't build a $14B debt stack without asset protection.

### 6. "NVIDIA will launch a buyback/trade-in program eventually."

**Counter:** NVIDIA currently offers only a 1-year standard warranty and has explicitly *removed* extended warranty options for GPUs (extended coverage is now limited to networking products only). They have zero enterprise buyback program. Even if they did, manufacturer programs historically cover trade-in credit, not guaranteed floor values — and they can be cancelled at any time. RVG is a binding contractual guarantee.

### 7. "Our fleet is too small / too large for this."

**Counter for small:** We price at the fleet level. Even 100 GPUs ($2.5M–$5.5M value) is insurable — and mid-tier operators are actually our sweet spot because you're less likely to self-insure. Counter for large: Portfolio scale works in your favor — diversification across GPU models and vintages reduces per-unit premium. CoreWeave-scale fleets (250,000 GPUs) are exactly the kind of portfolio where RVG economics are most compelling.

### 8. "We lease our GPUs — residual value isn't our problem."

**Counter:** If you're the *lessor*, residual value is exactly your problem — you own the asset at lease end. If you're the *lessee* on a triple-net lease (like xAI's Valor structure), your obligations include maintaining the asset value. Either way, someone in the chain needs residual protection.

### 9. "What happens if NVIDIA gets disrupted by AMD or custom silicon?"

**Counter:** That's precisely the scenario RVG exists for. If AMD's MI400 or Google's next TPU makes NVIDIA GPUs less competitive, residual values drop — and our policy pays the difference. We price this tail risk in. The question isn't whether disruption could happen; it's whether you can afford a $50M–$500M write-down when it does.

### 10. "We've never heard of GPU insurance — is this even a real product?"

**Counter:** Residual Value Insurance has been a real product since 1989. RVI Group has insured $59B in residuals across aircraft, vehicles, and commercial equipment with a 28–34% loss ratio. Tokio Marine HCC publishes RVG rates. The first GPU ABS already uses RV insurance as credit enhancement. What's new isn't the insurance structure — it's applying it to the fastest-growing asset class in history.

## 3B. Objections from Lenders & Structured Finance

### 1. "GPUs don't have a deep enough secondary market for us to rely on RVG."

**Counter:** The secondary market is growing rapidly — NVIDIA H100s and A100s trade actively through brokers, and A100 prices actually *increased* in early 2024 during the LLM demand surge. More importantly, aircraft RVI launched in the late 1980s when the aircraft secondary market was far less transparent than the GPU market is today. We're building the data infrastructure (condition scoring, price indexing, depreciation curves by model) that will become the Cirium Ascend of silicon.

### 2. "How do we value GPUs as collateral if there's no ISTAT equivalent?"

**Counter:** We're building it. Our GPU valuation framework uses thermal history, utilization hours, memory health metrics, and firmware compatibility as condition inputs — analogous to aircraft half-life assessments. For pricing, we track secondary market transactions, OEM list prices, and cloud rental rate proxies. Within 2–3 years, we'll have the data depth that took aviation 20 years to build, because GPU transaction velocity is orders of magnitude higher.

### 3. "The premium will eat into our deal economics."

**Counter:** The math works in your favor. A 4–10% RVG premium on the guaranteed amount, in exchange for AAA-tranche-enabling credit enhancement, reduces your blended cost of capital. The first GPU ABS achieved ~105bp on senior tranches with RV insurance — that spread compression more than covers the premium cost. RVG doesn't cost you money; it makes your deals cheaper.

### 4. "What if there's an AI winter — correlated loss across the entire GPU market?"

**Counter:** Correlated risk is real and we price for it. But consider: (a) RVI has survived multiple cycles — aircraft RVI weathered 9/11, the 2008 financial crisis, and COVID, with cumulative loss ratios still under 40%. (b) We use conservative attachment points (30–50% of current value) so we're covering the deep downside, not marginal depreciation. (c) Even in an "AI winter," GPUs retain utility for HPC, scientific computing, rendering, and inference — they don't go to zero the way single-purpose assets might.

### 5. "We need the insurer to be rated — who's behind this?"

**Counter:** We're backed by [reinsurance partner(s) — Munich Re, Swiss Re, Lloyd's syndicate capacity as applicable]. RVI Group (AM Best rated, $59B in insured residuals) and Tokio Marine HCC are the established capacity providers in the RVI market. Our reinsurance panel meets the rating requirements of any syndicated lending facility or ABS shelf.

### 6. "GPU values could go *up* — are we paying for insurance we'll never use?"

**Counter:** That's the best possible outcome — you paid a small premium and your collateral held its value. A100 prices did exactly this in early 2024. RVG is a put option — you only exercise it if things go wrong. The premium buys certainty, and certainty is what enables you to extend more credit at the same LTV.

### 7. "CoreWeave's 65% LTV covenant already protects us — we don't need RVG."

**Counter:** The LTV covenant protects you *if the borrower can maintain it*. If GPU values drop 40% across the fleet, the borrower is in breach and you're holding depreciating collateral in a down market. RVG puts a floor under the collateral value itself — it protects you even if the borrower can't. It's the difference between a covenant and a guarantee.

### 8. "We've done GPU deals without RVG — why start now?"

**Counter:** Early GPU deals were small enough to absorb residual risk. CoreWeave DDTL 1.0 was $2.3B. Now we're seeing $8.5B single tranches and $1.1B ABS shelves. At this scale, residual risk is systemic — one generation-skip event could impair billions across multiple deals. The market is institutionalizing, and institutional capital requires institutional risk transfer.

### 9. "How do you handle technology obsolescence risk in your pricing?"

**Counter:** We model NVIDIA's product roadmap as a known depreciation schedule overlay. Hopper (2022) → Blackwell (2024) → Rubin (2026) → next-gen (~2028). Each generational shift creates a predictable value step-down in prior generations. We price policies with generation-adjusted curves, not flat-line depreciation. The curve is steeper than aircraft but more predictable than many assume — NVIDIA publishes roadmaps 2–3 years out.

### 10. "What's the claims process? How do we trigger the guarantee?"

**Counter:** Same structure as aircraft RVI. At the policy's defined expiry date, an independent appraisal determines fair market value (FMV) of the GPU fleet. If FMV < insured floor value, we pay the deficiency. The GPU fleet must meet defined return conditions (documented maintenance, functional status, no undisclosed damage). For portfolio policies, we can structure either individual-asset or portfolio-aggregate triggers.

## 3C. Objections from Reinsurers

### 1. "There's no actuarial data for GPU depreciation curves."

**Counter:** There's more than you'd think. We have depreciation policies from 8+ public companies via SEC filings (ranging from 3 to 7 years). We have secondary market transaction data showing A100 price movements over 3+ years. Amazon's decision to shorten useful life to 5 years gives us a corporate signal. And the GPU ABS market's 40% RV floor is an institutional pricing benchmark. This is more data than aircraft RVI had when it launched in 1989.

### 2. "GPU technology cycles are too fast — we can't price 5-year risk."

**Counter:** Then price 2-year risk. Short-tenor policies are actually our sweet spot. A 2-year RVG on H100s, with NVIDIA's Blackwell and Rubin roadmap known, is highly modelable. As data accumulates, we extend tenors. This is exactly how RVI Group built the aircraft market — conservative, short-term policies first, expanding as loss history proved favorable.

### 3. "The correlation risk is unacceptable — all GPUs depreciate together."

**Counter:** Partially true, partially wrong. Correlation exists within the same GPU generation (all H100s are affected by Blackwell's launch). But across generations, use cases, and geographies, there's meaningful diversification. An H100 running inference in India has different demand dynamics than an A100 doing scientific computing in Europe. We structure portfolios across model/vintage/use-case to maximize diversification credit.

### 4. "NVIDIA could flood the secondary market with refurbished GPUs."

**Counter:** NVIDIA has no refurbishment or buyback program today. Their business model is selling new chips at premium margins — flooding the secondary market would cannibalize their own sales. If anything, NVIDIA's incentive is to *restrict* secondary supply (through firmware locks, warranty limitations) to drive new purchases. This structural dynamic actually supports residual values.

### 5. "The loss ratio will be much worse than aircraft RVI's 28–34%."

**Counter:** We agree — we're pricing for higher loss ratios. Our initial target is 40–55% loss ratio, which is still well below the 70–99% typical in P&C. Higher premiums (8–15% vs. aircraft's 4–7%) compensate. And the shorter policy terms mean faster loss development — we'll know within 2–3 years if our curves are accurate, vs. 10–15 years for aircraft. The feedback loop is tight.

### 6. "What if export controls or sanctions make entire GPU fleets stranded assets?"

**Counter:** Regulatory risk is real but bounded. We exclude sanctioned jurisdictions from coverage and price geopolitical risk by geography. US-deployed NVIDIA GPUs face minimal export control risk (they're already in-country). For international policies, we carve out or surcharge accordingly. This is analogous to how aircraft RVI handles political risk in emerging markets.

### 7. "Who's the loss adjuster? There are no GPU appraisers."

**Counter:** We're building the appraisal infrastructure. GPU condition can be objectively assessed through diagnostic data (memory health, thermal history, benchmark performance, firmware compatibility). For market value, we use a panel of secondary market dealers and track transaction data. The appraisal process is actually *easier* than aircraft because GPUs are commodity hardware — one H100 is interchangeable with another H100 in the same condition class.

### 8. "This could be a one-cycle market — if AI spending pulls back, there's no business."

**Counter:** Even in a pullback scenario, the installed base of GPUs doesn't disappear. The $90–120B in GPU assets on corporate balance sheets today will still need protection. And a demand pullback is precisely when RVG claims increase and the product proves its value — which drives adoption for the next cycle. RVI Group survived every market cycle since 1989.

### 9. "How much capacity do you need and what's the reinsurance structure?"

**Counter:** We're starting with $500M in total capacity, structured as quota share on a per-policy basis. Initial attachment at 30–50% of current GPU value, 2–3 year policy terms. As loss history develops, we'll move to excess-of-loss structures. We're targeting $50M–$100M per-risk limits initially, scaling to $500M+ as the book grows.

### 10. "What's our downside scenario — worst-case loss?"

**Counter:** Worst case: a simultaneous AI winter + NVIDIA architecture leap + secondary market collapse. In that scenario, GPU values could drop 60–70% from insured levels. On a $500M book at 50% attachment, maximum loss exposure is ~$150–175M (the gap between 50% floor and 20–30% trough value). That's within normal catastrophe tolerance for a specialty line. Our Monte Carlo models suggest 99th percentile loss at approximately 2.5× expected loss, which at target loss ratios of 45–55% puts the tail at ~115–140% loss ratio — painful but not capital-threatening.

### Structural Similarities That Matter

1. **Same economic function:** All three products guarantee a minimum future asset value, paying the deficiency between realized and insured value. The payout structure is identical.

2. **Same buyer motivation:** Asset-backed lenders and lessors in all three markets use RVI/RVG to reduce loss-given-default, achieve better credit ratings on securitizations, and expand lending capacity.

3. **Same actuarial framework:** Depreciation curve estimation → volatility modeling → Monte Carlo simulation → portfolio diversification credit → attachment point optimization. The math translates directly.

4. **Same market evolution path:** Aircraft RVI started in 1989 with a small number of lessors and conservative terms. Auto RVI scaled through fleet leasing. GPU RVG follows the same adoption curve — starting with neo-cloud operators and lenders, expanding to enterprise.

5. **GPU RVG has a *faster* feedback loop:** 2–3 year policy terms mean loss experience develops in years, not decades. This enables faster pricing refinement than aircraft (5–15 year policies) or auto (2–4 year but lower per-unit observation).

### Key Revenue Drivers

1. **GPU capex growth (30–50%/yr):** Every new GPU purchased is a potential insurable asset. The market is growing faster than almost any prior asset class.

2. **Financing penetration:** As GPU lending matures, RVG will become a standard requirement in loan documentation — just as casualty insurance already is. This creates mandatory demand.

3. **Rate adequacy:** Initial premiums (8–15%) reflect data uncertainty. As loss history develops, rates compress — but total premium grows because the book grows faster than rates decline.

4. **Retention improvement:** Year 1 requires heavy reinsurance (60%+ cession). As track record develops, Corgi retains more risk → higher margin.

5. **Data moat compounding:** Every policy written generates proprietary data on GPU condition, utilization, and realized residual values. This data improves pricing accuracy, which improves loss ratios, which attracts more reinsurance capacity, which enables more policies.
