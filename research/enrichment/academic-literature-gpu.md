# Academic Literature — GPU Depreciation & Technology Asset Residual Value

## Search 6: Moore's Law — Computing Asset Depreciation Economic Analysis

### Key Sources Found

1. **"Measuring Moore's Law: Evidence from Price, Cost, and Quality Indexes"**
   - *Authors:* Flamm, Kenneth
   - *Source:* NBER Working Paper No. 24553 (National Bureau of Economic Research), 2018
   - *Summary:* Empirically documents the slowdown in Moore's Law as measured by quality-adjusted chip prices across memory, foundry logic, and Intel microprocessors, and estimates semiconductor manufacturing equipment depreciation rates — finding that fab lives have lengthened as innovation rates slowed. For GPU RVG pricing, this paper justifies distinguishing between pre- and post-Moore's Law slowdown depreciation regimes, as the pace of value erosion differs structurally across GPU generations.

2. **"The Economic Impact of Moore's Law: Evidence from When It Faltered"**
   - *Authors:* Thompson, Neil
   - *Source:* Working paper (neil-t.com / conference publication), ~2017
   - *Summary:* Measures the TFP productivity losses (0.5–0.7 pp per year per standard deviation of chip-software mismatch) when Moore's Law stalled for specific firms, establishing that computing performance gains have direct, measurable economic value. Relevant to GPU RVG because it quantifies the *economic obsolescence* channel: GPUs depreciate not only in market price but in productive value when successor chips accelerate AI workloads, determining when rational lessees would exit a lease.

3. **"Moore's Law and the Economics of Semiconductor Price Trends"**
   - *Authors:* Various contributors
   - *Source:* National Academies Press (National Research Council symposium report), 2004
   - *Summary:* Establishes that Moore's Law–driven price declines played a major macroeconomic role in the 1990s productivity boom, and models the feedback between chip price trends and equipment investment cycles. Provides the long-run empirical context for GPU depreciation curves, demonstrating that historical price declines of ~30% per year in computing hardware are the structural baseline against which GPU RVG residual values must be calibrated.

4. **"Moore's Law and Its Practical Implications" — Managerial Note**
   - *Source:* CSIS / Saylor Academy management textbook
   - *Summary:* Notes Dell's anecdotal estimate that chip-based inventory depreciates ~1% per week (~40% annually), consistent with empirical secondary-market data for consumer and enterprise GPUs. Provides a useful rule-of-thumb depreciation rate that can anchor GPU RVG scenario planning before more granular market data is available.

## Search 5: Data Center Hardware Replacement Cycle — Optimal TCO

### Key Sources Found

1. **"Rearchitecting Datacenter Lifecycle for AI: A TCO-Driven Framework"**
   - *Authors:* Stojkovic, J. et al. (University of Illinois & Microsoft Azure Research)
   - *Source:* arXiv:2509.26534, September 2025
   - *Summary:* Proposes a holistic lifecycle management framework for AI datacenters that reduces TCO by up to 40% by coordinating building, hardware refresh, and operational decisions; finds extending AI hardware beyond 6 years is cost-effective in most scenarios, though dependent on GPU generational improvement rates. Directly informs GPU RVG underwriting: if lessees rationally hold hardware ≥6 years, the RVG risk window and optimal policy term become clearer, and the pace of generation-driven value erosion is the key variable.

2. **"Navigating Hardware Refresh Cycles in the Data Center"**
   - *Source:* Horizon Technology (horizontechnology.com), 2024
   - *Summary:* Industry analysis noting OEMs recommend 3–5 year refresh cycles while actual server operational lives average 7–10 years for rack servers, with Microsoft and hyperscalers extending cloud server lifespans from 4 to 6+ years. Relevant to GPU RVG because the gap between OEM-recommended and actual refresh timing defines the period during which residual value risk is most acute — and most insurable.

3. **"Fundamentals of Managing the Data Center Life Cycle for Owners"**
   - *Source:* Insight (Schneider Electric white paper), ongoing
   - *Summary:* Comprehensive data centre lifecycle framework covering capital expenditure planning, refresh-cycle economics, and maintenance cost trajectories across hardware generations. Provides the TCO decomposition structure needed to model the economic incentive for lessees to trigger (or not trigger) a GPU RVG claim at policy expiry.

## Search 4: Semiconductor Obsolescence Lifecycle Modeling

### Key Sources Found

1. **"Electronic Part Life Cycle Concepts and Obsolescence Forecasting"**
   - *Authors:* Sandborn, P. & Pecht, M. (CALCE Center, University of Maryland)
   - *Source:* *IEEE Transactions on Components and Packaging Technologies*, December 2000
   - *Summary:* Introduces a lifecycle curve model for electronic parts that separates technology-level obsolescence from part-number-level discontinuance, enabling probabilistic forecasting of years-to-obsolescence. Foundational methodology for GPU RVG: distinguishes between *Hopper architecture* becoming obsolete vs. a specific H100 SKU being discontinued — a distinction critical for pricing trigger clauses.

2. **"Electronic Part Obsolescence Forecasting Based on Time Series Modeling"**
   - *Authors:* Ma, J. & Kim, N.
   - *Source:* *International Journal of Precision Engineering and Manufacturing*, 2017 — DOI: 10.1007/s12541-017-0092-6
   - *Summary:* Proposes time-series methods (ARIMA-class) for forecasting when electronic parts will be discontinued, validated on historical component databases. Directly applicable to GPU RVG pricing as it provides a quantitative framework for estimating the probability of H100/H200 end-of-life within a 3–5 year policy window.

3. **"Life-cycle Assessment of Semiconductors"**
   - *Authors:* Boyd, Sarah B.
   - *Source:* UC Berkeley PhD Thesis (eScholarship), 2012
   - *Summary:* Compares semiconductor production and use-phase impacts across 7 CMOS technology generations, finding rapid shrinkage in per-chip lifecycle from ~30 years (1970) to 2–5 years (modern advanced nodes). Establishes the empirical basis for GPU RVG underwriters to assume a compressed functional lifetime relative to traditional capital equipment.

4. **"Effective Management of Semiconductor Obsolescence in Automotive ECU Manufacturers"**
   - *Authors:* Not specified
   - *Source:* *International Journal of Innovative Research* (IJFMR), 2025
   - *Summary:* Documents semiconductor EOL cycles of 7–10 years for automotive-grade components, contrasted with GPU/AI accelerator cycles of 2–3 years, quantifying the growing obsolescence management burden. Provides a comparative baseline showing GPU obsolescence is 3–5× faster than industrially managed components, a key RVG risk multiplier.

## Search 3: Aircraft Residual Value Insurance — Actuarial Methodology

### Key Sources Found

1. **"Residual Value Guaranty" — Product Overview**
   - *Source:* Tokio Marine HCC (tmhcc.com), ongoing
   - *Summary:* Defines the RVG insurance product structure: coverage of 30–80% of estimated residual value (or 20–40% of current value for aircraft), with premiums paid 100% upfront and policy durations of 5–15 years. Establishes the structural template directly applicable to a GPU RVG product, including coverage attachment point, co-insurance requirements, and lead-time for first-policy underwriting (~2–3 months).

2. **"Use of Residual-Value Insurance to Support Debt Repayment in Leveraged Aircraft Leases"**
   - *Authors:* Katten Muchin Rosenman LLP
   - *Source:* Katten legal publication / National Law Review, 2023
   - *Summary:* Documents how RVI policies are integrated into leveraged aircraft lease structures to increase lender pool size and reduce credit spreads for equity investors. Highlights co-insurance alignment mechanisms and the importance of asset-condition covenants — both transferable to GPU RVG contract design.

3. **"Residual Value Insurance" — ELFA Equipment Leasing and Finance Foundation**
   - *Authors:* Winter (first-author surname only in PDF title)
   - *Source:* Equipment Leasing and Finance Association (leasefoundation.org), academic white paper
   - *Summary:* Industry white paper covering RVI pricing factors including economic cycle correlation, GDP/interest rate sensitivity, and technological obsolescence — exactly the three macro risk drivers relevant to GPU RVG. Notes that assets best suited for RVI have longer economic lives and active secondary markets, flagging GPU-specific suitability concerns the product must address.

4. **"How RVI Works"**
   - *Source:* Asset RVI (assetrvi.com), practitioner resource
   - *Summary:* Explains the mechanics of RVI from attachment point selection to claims settlement, noting that pricing varies by attachment point, coverage amount, and portfolio diversification. Portfolio-level diversification effects are directly relevant to GPU RVG because diversifying across GPU generations, lessees, and end-use sectors reduces correlated obsolescence risk.

Flags the attribute-selection challenge that GPU RVG models must solve (architecture generation, utilisation hours, memory bandwidth retention, etc.).

## Search 1: GPU Depreciation Curve — NVIDIA H100 / A100 Resale Value (Empirical)

### Key Sources Found

1. **"The Illusion of Stability: Unpacking H100 GPU Market Value Trends"**
   - *Source:* Silicon Data (silicondata.com), ~2025
   - *Summary:* Empirical secondary-market analysis showing H100s held ~85% of new-unit value through month 18, then dropped sharply to 30–50% by months 18–24 and 20–35% by months 24–36. Directly relevant to GPU RVG as it provides observed depreciation curve breakpoints and the refurbished vs. used pricing differential (~15–25 pp) needed for residual value modelling.

2. **"How Fast Does an AI Chip Depreciate and Why Does It Matter for Nvidia Stock?"**
   - *Source:* Little Sioux Corn Processors / financial media syndication, 2024
   - *Summary:* Documents that rental-rate pricing for GPUs dropped ≥40% per year for small clusters, with NVIDIA's projected $4/GPU-hour revenue window collapsing in under 18 months. Useful for calibrating income-approach residual value benchmarks for H100 RVG pricing.

3. **"How Long Do GPUs Last Anyway? Hyperscalers' Depreciation Policies & GPUaaS Unit Economics"**
   - *Source:* Applied Conjectures (Substack), 2024
   - *Summary:* Reviews how major cloud providers (AWS, CoreWeave) set book-depreciation schedules for AI GPUs (typically 4–5 years straight-line) versus observed market-value decay. Highlights that CoreWeave's A100 and H100 fleets remained near-fully utilised in 2024, suggesting demand-side factors can offset pure technological obsolescence in the near term.

4. **"Secondary GPU Markets: Buying and Selling Used AI Hardware Guide 2025"**
   - *Source:* Introl Blog (introl.com), 2025
   - *Summary:* Practical guide to secondary-market dynamics for data-centre GPUs, noting optimal resale windows of 12–18 months post-purchase and sharp value cliffs when successor architectures launch. Reinforces the "generation-transition event" risk that RVG insurance products must price.

5. **"$2 H100s: How the GPU Bubble Burst"**
   - *Source:* Latent Space / Eugene Cheah (Substack), 2025
   - *Summary:* Traces the collapse of spot GPU rental rates from ~$4/hr to ~$2/hr as Hopper supply caught up with LLM demand, with used H100 units peaking at ~$50k in mid-2024 before falling sharply. Quantifies the speed and magnitude of value erosion that a residual-value guarantee product would need to cover.
